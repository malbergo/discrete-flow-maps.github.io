A framework for trajectory compression on the probability simplex

Discrete Flow Maps

The sequential nature of autoregressive next-token prediction imposes a fundamental speed limit on Large Language Models. While continuous flow models offer a path to parallel generation, they traditionally demand expensive iterative integration. Flow Maps bypass this bottleneck by compressing generative trajectories into single-step mappings. However, standard formulations rely on Euclidean regression losses that are geometrically ill-suited for discrete data. We resolve this conflict with Discrete Flow Maps, a framework that reconciles trajectory compression with the geometry of the probability simplex.

The Probability Simplex

Consider a vocabulary of \(K\) tokens—for instance, three words: fox, dog, and cat.

Each token is represented as a one-hot vector \(e_i \in \mathbb{R}^K\), forming the vertices of the \((K{-}1)\)-dimensional probability simplex \(\Delta^{K-1}\).

Any probability distribution over the vocabulary is a point inside the simplex. The closer a point is to a vertex, the higher the probability assigned to that token.

The simplex is defined as:

\[\Delta^{K-1} = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^K : x \geq 0,\; \langle \mathbf{1}, x \rangle = 1\}\]

The Instantaneous Denoiser

At any time \(s\) along the generative process, given a noisy state \(I_s = x\), we can ask: what is the posterior probability that the clean data is each token?

The answer is the instantaneous denoiser \(\psi_{s,s}(x)\)—a weighted sum of vertices, where the weight on each vertex \(e_i\) is the posterior probability \(p_i = \mathbb{P}(I_1 = e_i \mid I_s = x)\).

Since each weight is non-negative and they sum to one, the denoiser is always a valid probability distribution—it lives on the simplex by construction:

\[\psi_{s,s}(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \mathbb{P}(I_1 = e_i \mid I_s = x)\, e_i = \mathbb{E}[I_1 \mid I_s = x]\]

This is simply the conditional expectation of the clean data given the noisy observation.

The Mean Denoiser

The generative ODE traces a trajectory from random noise \(x_0\) to a data point \(x_1\) (a simplex vertex). Along this trajectory, the instantaneous denoiser \(\psi_{s,s}\) evolves continuously.

We mark two points along the trajectory: an earlier time \(x_s\) and a later time \(x_t\). The tangent at \(x_s\) projects onto the simplex at the instantaneous denoiser \(\psi_{s,s}\).

As we integrate along the trajectory from \(s\) to \(t\), we define the mean denoiser \(\psi_{s,t}\)—a time-weighted average of the instantaneous denoisers along the path:

\[\psi_{s,t}(x_s) = \int_s^t w(u)\,\mathbb{E}[I_1 \mid I_u = x_u]\, du\]

Key insight: Since each \(\mathbb{E}[I_1 \mid I_u = x_u]\) is on the simplex, and \(w(u)\) integrates to 1, the mean denoiser \(\psi_{s,t}\) is also always on the simplex. This enables cross-entropy losses for flow map training.

The secant between \(x_s\) and \(x_t\) on the trajectory corresponds to the mean denoiser \(\psi_{s,t}\) on the simplex. The projection from the trajectory onto the simplex is the geometric bridge that connects the flow map to discrete losses.

The Discrete Flow Map

The flow map \(X_{s,t}\) jumps directly from time \(s\) to time \(t\) along the generative trajectory. We parameterize it as a convex combination of the current state and the mean denoiser:

\[X_{s,t}(x_s) = \frac{1-t}{1-s}\,x_s + \frac{t-s}{1-s}\,\psi_{s,t}(x_s)\]

Since \(\psi_{s,t}\) lives on the simplex, the flow map output is a convex combination of \(x_s\) and a point on the simplex. At \(t = 1\), the map yields the full generation: \(X_{s,1}(x_s) = \psi_{s,1}(x_s)\), a point on the simplex.

This parameterization is the key to our framework: it replaces unconstrained Euclidean regression with geometrically consistent objectives on the simplex.

Semi-Group Consistency (PSD)

The flow map must satisfy the semi-group property: jumping from \(s\) to \(t\) must equal jumping from \(s\) to \(u\), then from \(u\) to \(t\).

In terms of the mean denoiser, this becomes a convex decomposition:

\[\psi_{s,t}(x_s) = \alpha_{s,u,t}\,\psi_{s,u}(x_s) + \beta_{s,u,t}\,\psi_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(x_s))\]

Since both sides are probability distributions (points on the simplex), we can enforce this identity via KL divergence rather than L\(^2\) regression:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{PSD}} = \mathbb{E}\Big[D_{\text{KL}}\big(\text{sg}[\alpha\,\hat\psi_{s,u} + \beta\,\hat\psi_{u,t}(\hat X_{s,u})]\;\|\;\hat\psi_{s,t}\big)\Big]\]

Lagrangian Consistency (LSD)

The Lagrangian perspective requires that the flow endpoint velocity matches the instantaneous drift. In terms of the mean denoiser:

\[\psi_{s,t}(x_s) = \psi_{t,t}(x_t) - C_{s,t}\,\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi_{s,t}(x_s)\]

To obtain a valid target distribution, we derive an equivalent condition in logit space:

\[\psi_{s,t} = \text{Softmax}\!\big(z_{t,t}(x_t) - \log(\mathbf{1} + C_{s,t}(\dot z_{s,t} - \bar{\dot z}_{s,t}\mathbf{1}))\big)\]

This logit-space formulation always yields a valid probability distribution, enabling KL divergence training.

Eulerian Consistency (ESD)

The Eulerian perspective enforces that the flow map is invariant to the source time—if you re-parameterize where you start, the endpoint should not change:

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial s}\psi_{s,t}(x) + J_x\psi_{s,t}(x)\,b_s(x) = \kappa_{s,t}\big(\psi_{s,t}(x) - \psi_{s,s}(x)\big)\]

Again, we derive an equivalent logit-space condition:

\[\psi_{s,t} = \text{Softmax}\!\big(z_{s,s}(x) - \log(\mathbf{1} - \kappa_{s,t}^{-1}(D_s z_{s,t} - \overline{D_s z_{s,t}}\,\mathbf{1}))\big)\]

All three consistency losses—PSD, LSD, and ESD—are exact objectives derived directly from the geometric definitions of the flow, with no approximations.

Differential Semi-Group (dPSD)

Taking the derivative of the semi-group condition with respect to the intermediate time \(u\) yields a fourth identity:

\[\frac{d}{du}\big[\alpha_{s,u,t}\,\psi_{s,u}(x) + \beta_{s,u,t}\,\psi_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(x))\big] = 0\]

Setting \(u = s\) recovers ESD; setting \(u = t\) recovers LSD. This unifies all three consistency rules as special cases of one differential identity.

Parallel Generation

Unlike autoregressive models that generate one token at a time, discrete flow maps operate on all \(L\) token positions simultaneously.

Each position has its own simplex. The flow map transports noise to data across all positions in parallel, enabling generation of an entire sequence in a single forward pass.

The flows from Gaussian noise converge to simplex vertices—each vertex representing a token in the vocabulary. The full sequence emerges simultaneously rather than sequentially.

Reward Fine-Tuning

A pre-trained discrete flow map may not satisfy downstream objectives out of the box. For instance, a base model might produce an incorrect arithmetic result.

The base (green) dynamics land on tokens that form a plausible but wrong answer.

Fine-tuning steers the flow map dynamics toward a reward signal. The adapted (red) trajectories are nudged so that the landing tokens produce the correct answer.

Because each position is independent, steering respects the parallel structure—all positions are corrected simultaneously.
